The changelog

Every release, in plain English. If you reported a bug and it's fixed, you'll find it here by name. Newest first — and remember, the app tells you when a newer build than yours exists.

23 JULY 2026 · REV I

The guidance era

  • Let the Wizard Guide You. Tell the app what you shot — one optional question on step 1 (colour camera? dualband filter? mono narrowband?) — and a gold trail of suggested clicks lights the way through every step, tuned to your data type. Follow the gold and you can’t get lost; ignore it and nothing nags. The mono combiner asks its own version of the question and even relabels its slots (“S → red, H → green, O → blue”) — the eternal which-file-goes-where question, answered where it’s asked.
  • The Wizard’s Way: see both, pick one. The gold-bordered button on the Big Stretch (and step 1) fills the screen with two live previews — linked and unlinked, side by side, neutrally labelled — and you simply click the one that looks right. On the stretch page the panes show your actual stretch both ways, so what you pick is exactly what you get; changing your mind afterwards re-applies instantly.
  • Wizard’s Curves — Colour Transmutation Spells. Five of the wizard’s own finishing curves as one-click stackable spells: Yellow to Copper & Gold, Blue to Teal, Dynamic Pop, Add Warmth, Cool Down. And every spell carries a 📖 button that opens the recipe inside the Curves window, fully editable — see the move, learn the move, make it yours.
  • Deep learned a second stance: “keep colours”. Classic Deep balances each channel itself (perfect for palettes and neutral skies); the new sibling digs just as deep while preserving your field’s true colour balance — golden Milky Way fields stay golden.
  • Normal is stronger now (150, the community-classic value). If you loved the gentler look, Custom 50 recreates it exactly — the Custom dial even marks the spot, and it now travels logarithmically so equal drag means equal visual change.
  • Auto Colour Balance learned some humility. On fields whose brightest stars are genuinely warm, it used to “correct” honest star colour into a full green cast (found the hard way, twice, on the same stubborn dust field). Corrections are now bounded to ±12% — a field’s true colour is not a calibration error — and a lurking maths bug that could blank a channel died in the same surgery.
  • Flow, everywhere: the step numbers up top are clickable; going back asks whether to rewind or keep your image as-is; finished steps offer a gold “Done here — Next Step”; already-applied steps warn before you double-run an AI tool; and the live console is on by default (it’s the app’s black box recorder — off-switch in Settings).
  • Every feature above was forged in marathon field-testing by the wizard himself — including debugging one of his own spells with the 📖 button an hour after it was built.

20 JULY 2026 · REV D

The one that digs deeper — and learns spells

  • The Deep Stretch. Last release we promised “round one” on very faint data — this is round two, and it’s a new engine. Instead of stretching harder (which, we measured, just brightens the sky), Deep anchors to your data: it solves its own white point, black point and strength from the image itself. Faint dust fields open fully, M51-class galaxies reveal their tidal halos, and bright cores stay unblown. Thanks to Lee Polikoff, whose unstretchable stack started this whole hunt. One honest note, printed right on the button: Deep digs up everything — a blotchy background means flaws in the data (flats, gradients), not a bug.
  • Wizard’s Curves — Colour Transmutation Spells. The wizard’s own finishing curves, straight from years of tutorials, now one-click and stackable: Yellow to Copper & Gold (faint yellows go copper, bright stay gold), Blue to Teal (cores pull to OIII teal), and Dynamic Pop (the gentle finishing S-curve). More spells will join the book over time.
  • Stretch wisdom, updated by experiment: with data that came through the earlier steps, stretch linked first — whatever your data type. The wizard A/B-tested his own long-taught “dualband wants unlinked” rule and the linked path won: deeper faint extensions, subtler colour. The tip lives right next to the switch now.
  • The stretch panel got simpler and smarter — Gentle retired (Custom covers it), the Custom dial now travels logarithmically with Normal and Aggressive marked on it in plain English, and if a stretch comes out genuinely flat, the app quietly points at Deep instead of leaving you guessing.
  • Navigate like you own the place: the step numbers up top are now clickable; going back asks whether to rewind your image or keep it as-is (crop a spot off a finished image without redoing five steps); and revisiting a step whose work is already in your image gets a clear “already applied” warning — no more accidental double-sharpening.
  • The live console is now on by default — it’s become the app’s black box recorder and bug-report machine (off-switch in Settings, if you must).

18 JULY 2026 · REV K

The all-night Mac stakeout

  • The real Mac bug, caught red-handed. Last release we woke the app from its nap — a true fix, but not the whole story. This time, armed with his own MacBook and Apple's diagnostic tools at 3am, Luke caught the deeper culprit in the act: on modern macOS, the app's “refresh the screen” call could get trapped re-drawing the window forever, so it never noticed GraXpert had finished — minutes ago. The interface looked alive; the part that watches for results wasn't. Fixed for good. Field numbers from the stakeout: background extraction 3 seconds, denoise 34 seconds — steps that used to “hang” for 20+ minutes. Mike Frazier's zoom-trick observation was the clue that cracked both chapters of this saga — poking the window was literally breaking the spell.
  • Denoising switched to GraXpert's 2.0.0 AI model on every platform — dramatically faster (about 40× on Apple Silicon), with output quality we couldn't tell apart from the newer model in side-by-side tests. Same model everywhere, so tutorials match your results whatever you run.
  • The Big Stretch reaches deeper into very faint stacks — data scaled into the bottom sliver of the numeric range (thanks to Lee Polikoff's file) used to come out as flat grey mud at any strength; the white-point logic now recognises real signal and opens it up. Honest note: this is round one. On the deepest data there's still more in the tank than a 1000-strength stretch pulls out — deeper reach is actively in the works.
  • One engine at a time, and the app says so — starting a second AI tool or changing steps mid-run now gets a friendly “hang tight” instead of a silent gamble with your working image. The console also narrates honestly: when the result file starts landing on disk, how big it's grown, and exactly when it's collected.
  • Quieter and tougher — the app no longer burns a full CPU core while waiting on an engine, never mistakes an old output file for a fresh one, and if its own plumbing ever fails, the full error prints right in the console ready to copy-paste to us.

17 JULY 2026 · REV B

The one where the app was asleep

  • The great Mac mystery, solved — the app was literally napping. macOS puts idle-looking apps into “App Nap”, and waiting for GraXpert is exactly when AstroWizard looks idle. GraXpert finished its work fine; AstroWizard dozed until you clicked something — which is why zooming or pressing Next Step made runs “suddenly complete”. The app now opts out of App Nap entirely. Full credit to Mike Frazier, whose zoom-trick observation cracked the case, and Rick, who called it exactly: “AstroWizard is not seeing the processing has completed.”
  • Stackers with messy headers can't break saves anymore — malformed header cards (like ASTAP's broken CALSTAT) are now repaired on the fly instead of aborting your crop. Thanks shreddybear.
  • macOS quarantine check: if GraXpert.app still carries the downloaded-app flag, the console now says so before running, with the exact one-line cure. Thanks Greg.
  • Windows shortcut trap: pointing Settings at a desktop shortcut (.lnk) instead of the real program now gets an orange warning and plain instructions, instead of a cryptic “not a valid win32 application”.

17 JULY 2026

The night-shift build

  • Linux previews work now. A library the image viewer needed wasn’t bundled, so Linux showed “No module named PIL._tkinter_finder” instead of your image. Fixed — with thanks to David Huff and johnsteed for the pinpoint reports.
  • The Linux file picker shows extensionless tools — no more renaming GraXpert to .sh just to select it.
  • RC-Astro no longer fails on decorated filenames. Files named like “NGC 6992, 2025-08-23, (CV)…” — which is most stacker exports — broke BlurXTerminator with a cryptic “cfitsio status 366”. AstroWizard now keeps its working filenames free of the characters that trigger it. Thanks Pawel!
  • Macs can finally pick GraXpert properly. The old file dialog often refused to select .app bundles — one tester’s path ended up pointing at macOS’s own “Apps” launcher, which AstroWizard then dutifully ran instead of GraXpert (thanks Brian — your report solved it, and you were never the problem). The Mac picker is now a folder chooser that accepts GraXpert.app or its folder, and any path that doesn’t look like the right tool shows orange in Settings with an explanation.

16 JULY 2026

The comment-section build

  • GraXpert on Macs now talks. Its progress and error messages were being silently swallowed on macOS — they now stream into the console live, and AstroWizard collects its finished result wherever and however it saves it (including Seestar-style .fit files). Built directly from console evidence sent in by launch-day testers — thank you Michael, Greg, Rob, and Brian.
  • The Settings window no longer multiplies when clicked repeatedly — which also fixes chosen paths appearing not to save.
  • One-click bug reports: the console gained a Copy button. If something misbehaves, Show Console → Copy → paste it in a comment.
  • Final noise touch-ups grew to a full set: Light and Normal strengths for both GraXpert and NXT (thanks JustJoe).
  • The Step-1 tip now matches the renamed “Image looks all one colour?” switch.
  • Version numbers finally agree everywhere — Finder, Settings, and this website all show the same build date.
  • Install guide covers macOS Sequoia (Apple removed the right-click → Open trick; it’s System Settings → Privacy & Security → Open Anyway now).

15 JULY 2026

The Mac fix, properly this time

  • The real cure for GraXpert hanging on Macs: it turns out GraXpert was finishing its work and saving the result all along — its process just never exited afterwards, so the progress bar bounced forever over a completed job. AstroWizard now watches for the finished file, collects it, and tidies up the leftover process itself. (Thanks to Greg for the detective work — his spot of a double file extension cracked the case.)
  • Output files no longer end up named .fits.fits.
  • Right-click now removes Curves points on Macs (Macs report right-clicks differently; Control-click works too).
  • The green-cast toggle is now “Image looks all one colour?” — it fixes red and blue casts just as well.
  • The preview no longer resizes when processing starts and ends — the progress bar has a permanent home.
  • Multi-pass noise reduction now shows “pass 1 of 2” as it works.
  • The Mac app’s version number now shows the build date instead of an eternal 1.0.0.

14 JULY 2026

The Apple Silicon fix

  • GraXpert no longer hangs on Apple Silicon Macs (all M-series chips). AstroWizard was accidentally passing its own internal library settings to GraXpert on launch; GraXpert loaded the wrong libraries and froze. Standalone GraXpert was never the problem — your setups were fine all along.
  • The window now fits smaller screens. On 13″ laptops the default window was wider than the display, hiding the Settings button off the right edge.

13 JULY 2026

Zero-click setup

  • AstroWizard now finds GraXpert and the RC-Astro tools by itself on standard installs — checked at every startup, so most people never need the Locate buttons at all.
  • Selected a folder instead of the program? It now works that out and finds the right file inside.
  • Clearer wording throughout setup: rc-astro is one file that runs all three tools — no need to hunt for three programs.

12 JULY 2026

The app that updates itself (politely)

  • Update notice: at startup AstroWizard checks this website for a newer build and shows a small banner if one exists. There's an off switch in Settings, and a promise printed next to it: nothing about you or your images is ever sent.
  • Your build date now shows in Settings — no more guessing which version you have.
  • ASTAP-stacked files no longer load as a pure white screen (different stacking programs use different pixel ranges; AstroWizard now handles them all).
  • Masked Saturation and Make it Pop are gentler per feedback, with a new Custom Strength dial for both (1.0× = the classic look).
  • GraXpert problems now explain themselves: its own messages appear in the console, crashes are detected and reported instead of bouncing the progress bar forever, and first-time-setup advice is offered when its AI models are missing.
  • PixInsight-stacked files no longer slow the RC-Astro tools to a crawl — huge file headers were choking them; AstroWizard now tidies these automatically (and the underlying bug was fixed by RC-Astro too, from our bug report).

9 JULY 2026

The stretch overhaul

  • New stretch engine: the linear preview adapts itself to your data, and the Gentle / Normal / Aggressive buttons were rebalanced to be meaningfully different (with honest labels).
  • Star-rich targets like the Pleiades now stretch properly instead of vanishing into black.
  • The green-cast toggle resets between images so it can't quietly discolour your next photo.
  • Tool windows (Curves, Tune, Mask Reach) resize with the window.
  • First solid Apple Silicon build (native, no Rosetta) with the Settings window fixed.

Before the website existed, the open beta ran through early July via YouTube community posts — highlights included the Curves editor with colour targeting, the narrowband Tune controls, star-removal workflows, and more fixes than anyone should admit to needing.
